## Highlights

### 1. PlantUML first release — diagram-as-code goes mainstream

**PlantUML** was first released on **17 April 2009** by Arnaud Roques, turning a concise text DSL into UML diagrams (and over time dozens of other types) with Graphviz handling layout. It solved the agile problem of keeping diagrams in sync with code by making the diagram *be* a text file — diffable, reviewable, embeddable in IDEs, wikis, and CI — and remains the canonical diagrams-as-code tool of its generation. See `REF-PLANTUML-001`.

### 2. Signavio founded

Incorporated 4 May 2009 in Berlin by four Hasso-Plattner-Institut alumni — builders of HPI's Oryx, the world's first web-based BPMN modeler. Signavio became the flagship "BPMN in the cloud" vendor of the BPM wave (acquired by SAP in 2021 for ~€1B).

### 3. Cacoo launches

Nulab (Fukuoka, Japan) launched **Cacoo** in November 2009, a browser diagram editor whose differentiator was *simultaneous multi-user real-time editing* — ahead of most Western rivals — covering wireframes, sitemaps, network diagrams, flowcharts and UML; it passed 1M users within a few years.

## Context

Where WebSequenceDiagrams did one diagram type, PlantUML covered the whole UML set and beyond. Its longevity — still widely used 15+ years later — is the strongest evidence that text-as-diagram was the right bet. Stanford's Protovis (April 2009), D3's direct ancestor, also shipped this year.

## Highlights

### 1. SVG 1.1 becomes a W3C Recommendation; UML 2.0 adopted

On **14 January 2003**, **SVG 1.1** reached Recommendation status alongside the Mobile profiles SVG Tiny and SVG Basic — the version most tooling actually targeted for the next decade. The same year the OMG adopted the **UML 2.0 Superstructure** as a Final Adopted Specification, expanding UML from ~9 to 13+ diagram types (composite-structure, timing, interaction-overview); its formal publication came in 2005.

### 2. Inkscape forks from Sodipodi

First release 0.35 on 2 November 2003. Four Sodipodi developers forked specifically to pursue **full SVG-standard compliance** — creating the open-source editor that made hand-authored and tool-authored SVG (the era's new diagram interchange format) universally editable.

### 3. The UML-tools consolidation completes

Within weeks of each other, IBM closed the Rational acquisition (February 2003) and Borland closed TogetherSoft (January 2003). Both leading independent UML vendors disappeared into platform companies — ending the standalone CASE-tool era and opening the gap that low-cost (Sparx EA) and open-source/Eclipse-based modeling filled.

## Context

The UML 2.0 dates are commonly muddled: **adopted 2003, published July 2005**. SVG 1.1's clean January date makes it the firmer single headline. SVG 1.1's modular, profile-based approach — implement subsets, extend to mobile — is the pattern that broadened where diagrams could render.

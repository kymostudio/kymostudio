## Highlights

### 1. UML 0.9 and the "Three Amigos" / UML Partners consortium

In June 1996 Rational published **Unified Modeling Language v0.9**, re-scoping the effort from a full *method* to a *notation/language* and renaming it UML. Ivar Jacobson joined Booch and Rumbaugh to complete the **"Three Amigos,"** folding in his use-case work, and Rational convened the international **UML Partners** consortium (IBM, HP, Microsoft, Oracle and others) to finish the spec and answer an OMG Request for Proposals. Narrowing from "method" to "language" was the decision that made UML adoptable by everyone; the consortium turned competitors into co-authors.

### 2. Visio Technical 4.1 pushes diagramming into engineering territory

Released 17 June 1996 with 500+ new SmartShapes (electrical, HVAC, plumbing, mechanical, software design) and batch AutoCAD conversion, **Visio Technical 4.1** positioned diagramming as a cheap alternative to CAD for technical drawing — starting Visio's segmentation into Standard / Technical / Professional product lines.

### 3. The BPR-era process-mapping boom peaks: 23 flowcharting packages in one buyer's guide

Quality Digest's March 1996 buyer's guide catalogued **23 flowcharting/process-mapping packages** — basic flowcharters (allCLEAR, CorelFlow, EasyFlow, Inspiration, RFFlow…) plus methodology-driven tools (BPwin/IDEF0, ProCap/IDEF3, Optima!, TeamFlow). A contemporary snapshot proving that *process* diagrams, not software models, were the mid-90s mainstream.

## Context

Jacobson's arrival mattered for diagrams specifically: use-case diagrams entered UML through him. The OMG RFP set a public, vendor-neutral finish line, which forced the spec toward completeness rather than Rational's convenience.

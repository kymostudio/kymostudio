## Highlights (projected)

### 1. Provenance & trust layers enter the tools list

A category whose product is an answer to two questions: *who or what asserted this view, from which sources, when* — and *is it still true?* Signed views, freshness attestations, and source-chain inspection become product surface. The C2PA generation solved this for images because generated media forced it; diagrams need it for the same reason, one era later.

### 2. The stale-view incident is the new outage genre

The failure mode that drives adoption: decisions made on views that were correct when rendered and wrong by the time they were acted on — or views derived from a source an agent had quietly mis-modeled. Postmortems start carrying a "view provenance" section, and trust tooling moves from nice-to-have to incident-driven priority.

### 3. Behavior maps reach the types top 4

Model-behavior maps pass the workflow graph: in organizations full of derived views, the picture humans study hardest is the one showing *how the machines that derive everything else actually behave* — capability boundaries, failure modes, drift since last assessment.

## Context

Era E's second half theme: authority created the trust problem. When diagrams were human-drawn, trust was social (you knew who drew it). Derived views need trust to be *technical* — and a category exists exactly where a social mechanism gets replaced by an infrastructural one.

## Highlights

### 1. UML begins: the "Unified Method" 0.8 at OOPSLA

At OOPSLA '95 in Austin, Grady Booch and Jim Rumbaugh — by then both at Rational Software — released the **version 0.8 draft of the "Unified Method"** (October 1995), merging Booch's method with Rumbaugh's Object Modeling Technique (OMT), the two most popular object-oriented modeling notations of the era. Object-oriented design was drowning in incompatible notations — Booch clouds, OMT, Shlaer-Mellor, Coad-Yourdon — so a diagram could not travel between teams. This was the seed of UML and the start of the consolidation that would, within two years, give the industry one shared visual language.

### 2. Visio 4.0 ships for Windows 95; Shapeware becomes Visio Corp and IPOs

Shapeware released **Visio 4.0** on 18 August 1995 as one of the first applications built specifically for Windows 95; in November the company renamed itself **Visio Corporation** and IPO'd on Nasdaq. This cemented drag-and-drop "SmartShapes" diagramming as a mainstream office software category, distinct from CAD and draw programs — the desktop-diagramming pole for the next two decades.

### 3. Rational acquires Objectory; Ivar Jacobson joins Booch and Rumbaugh

In October 1995 Ericsson divested Objectory AB to Rational Software, bringing use-case inventor **Ivar Jacobson** into the same company as Booch and Rumbaugh — the precondition for the "Three Amigos" unification. The three competing OO notations (Booch, OMT, Objectory/use cases) were now on a deliberate collision-merge course.

## Context

The pairing was new: Rumbaugh had only joined Booch at Rational in October 1994. The name was still "Unified **Method**" — the reframing to a *language* came in 1996. Meanwhile the commercial diagramming market was overwhelmingly about business process and data, not software models: the BPR/quality-management wave made flowcharting the volume category.

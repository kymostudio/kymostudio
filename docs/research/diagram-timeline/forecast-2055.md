## Highlights (projected)

### 1. The sixty-year arc, read end to end

1995: a diagram is a file on one person's desktop, drawn in Visio, meaningful only with its author in the room. 2055: a diagram is a signed, derived, replayable view of a live system — or a ratified map of what that system is allowed to intend — legible to anyone and acted on by things that are not people. Seven forms, each kept alive by the next: file, standard, web document, shared canvas, structured datum, derived view, agreement.

### 2. Flowchart, still top-3, sixty-one years on

The timeline's longest thread: #1 for its first 46 years, never below #3 since. Every era found a new reason to draw boxes and arrows in reading order — BPR, BPM, onboarding, agent workflows, explanations to one's future self. If this forecast errs everywhere else, it is most confident here: in 2085 someone will still be drawing a flowchart, and it will still be the first diagram a child learns.

### 3. The open question for 2056–2085

This timeline's eras were each defined by *where the diagram lives*. The closing question is different in kind: **what does a diagram mean when most of its readers are not human?** When machine systems negotiate directly in structured models, the rendered picture survives wherever a human must understand, ratify, or contest — which is to say the diagram's future is exactly as large as the human role in the systems it depicts. That is not a question about graphics.

## Highlights

### 1. PGML vs VML at the W3C — the SVG Working Group forms

Two rival XML vector-graphics proposals landed months apart: **PGML** (Adobe, IBM, Netscape, Sun; April 1998) and **VML** (Microsoft, HP, Macromedia, Autodesk, Visio; May 1998). The clash — among six competing submissions — pushed the W3C to form the **SVG Working Group**, which set out to build one new language "informed by, but not based on" any single proposal. This is the genesis of SVG, the format that would become the universal way to render diagrams on the web.

### 2. Open-source diagramming begins: Dia 0.1 and ArgoUML

Alexander Larsson announced **Dia 0.1** (GTK/GNOME, a free Visio-style editor) in August 1998; the same year Jason Robbins released **ArgoUML**, a Java open-source UML tool from his UC Irvine PhD work (100k+ downloads by mid-2001). Together they opened the free-software front in a category that had been entirely commercial.

### 3. The graph-drawing field gets its canonical textbook

Di Battista, Eades, Tamassia & Tollis published *Graph Drawing: Algorithms for the Visualization of Graphs* (Prentice Hall, 1998) — the first comprehensive treatment of layered (Sugiyama), force-directed, orthogonal and planarity-based layout, codifying the algorithms every later auto-layout engine (Graphviz, yFiles, ELK, dagre) draws on.

## Context

VML lived on for years inside Microsoft Office's drawing layer; PGML's ideas flowed into SVG. The "build new, don't pick a side" decision is why SVG borrowed from PostScript/PDF and CSS rather than crowning one vendor's format.

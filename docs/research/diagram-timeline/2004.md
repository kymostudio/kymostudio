## Highlights

### 1. BPMN 1.0 released by BPMI

On **3 May 2004**, the Business Process Management Initiative released **BPMN 1.0** — edited by Stephen A. White (IBM) — giving the business-process world a single standard graphical notation: pools, lanes, events, gateways, and sequence/message flows. BPMN did for *process* diagrams what UML did for *software* models, and became the dominant process-modeling standard worldwide.

### 2. Eclipse UML2 1.0: the UML metamodel becomes open-source infrastructure

Released June 2004 with Eclipse 3.0, the EMF-based implementation of the UML 2.x metamodel turned the standard itself into a freely reusable library — the substrate for a generation of open and commercial modeling tools (Papyrus, RSA, Topcased). The "modeling stack" equivalent of what SVG did for rendering.

### 3. Concept mapping institutionalizes: first CMC conference, CmapTools at its center

The first International Conference on Concept Mapping (Pamplona, September 2004) put IHMC's free **CmapTools** — already in mass use in education — at the center of a now-formalized field. A marker that *non-engineering* diagramming (knowledge/mind/concept maps, alongside FreeMind and MindManager) had become a software category of its own.

## Context

BPMN's later move to the OMG (2005–2006) and the BPMN 2.0 execution semantics (2010/2011) turned it from a drawing standard into a *portable, runnable* one — diagrams that workflow engines could execute directly. BPMN remains a first-class citizen in kymo via its importer.

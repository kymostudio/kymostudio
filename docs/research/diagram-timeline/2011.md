## Highlights

### 1. draw.io (Diagramly) launches; BPMN 2.0 finalized

JGraph launched its hosted mxGraph web app as **Diagramly** in 2011 (rebranded draw.io in 2012) — free, client-side, storing files in the user's own cloud, no lock-in. It became the default "just give me a diagram editor" for millions. The same year **BPMN 2.0** reached finalization (OMG lists December 2010; widely cited as early 2011), adding execution semantics and the DI interchange format that made process diagrams portable and runnable. See `REF-DRAWIO-001`.

### 2. D3.js released

Bostock, Ogievetsky and Heer released **D3 ("Data-Driven Documents")** in February 2011 as the successor to Protovis, binding data directly to the DOM over web standards (SVG/CSS). It became the foundational library for bespoke node-link, tree, flow and graph visualizations on the web — the data-driven end of the diagram spectrum and one of the most-starred JS projects ever.

### 3. RealtimeBoard (later Miro) founded

Andrey Khusid and Oleg Shardin founded **RealtimeBoard** in Perm, Russia (product launched publicly 2012), to turn their design agency's client-communication pain into an infinite collaborative whiteboard — the start of the freeform-whiteboard category that, as Miro, later reached a $17.5B valuation. AWS also published its official "Simple Icons" set around this time, starting standardized cloud-architecture diagramming.

## Context

draw.io's later rebrand to diagrams.net (2020) and its security-first, on-prem story kept it relevant into the AI era. BPMN 2.0's DI metamodel is what makes round-tripping BPMN between tools possible — and is exactly what kymo's `from_bpmn.py` consumes.
